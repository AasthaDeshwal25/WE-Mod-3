teach me yahtzee game
Yahtzee is a fun dice game that can be played with friends, family, or even solo! Here's a rundown of how to play:

What you'll need:

Five dice
Yahtzee scorecards (you can find these online or print them out)
Dice cup (optional, but helpful for shaking the dice)
Number of players:

Yahtzee can be played with 1 or more players, but it's best suited for 2-4 players.

Objective:

The goal of Yahtzee is to roll the dice and score points by filling in the boxes on your scorecard with specific dice combinations. The player with the highest score at the end of the game wins!

Gameplay:

Decide who goes first: You can do this by rolling the dice and whoever gets the highest total goes first. Play then proceeds clockwise.

Taking your turn:

On your turn, you get to roll the dice up to three times.
First roll: Roll all five dice. Decide which dice you want to keep (dice that contribute to your desired scoring combination) and set them aside. You can choose to stop rolling here and score based on your first roll, or you can move on to the second roll.
Second and third rolls: Re-roll any or all of the dice you didn't keep from the previous roll. Again, you can decide to stop and score after any roll.
Scoring:

After your final roll, you must choose a box on your scorecard to fill in. Each box corresponds to a specific scoring combination (e.g., three of a kind, full house, Yahtzee).
You can only fill in each box once per game.
If you can't form any of the scoring combinations, you must mark a zero in one of the boxes.
Scoring details:

The scorecard is divided into two sections: upper section and lower section.

Upper section: This section rewards you for scoring points based on the value of individual dice (aces, twos, threes, etc.).
Lower section: This section rewards you for specific dice combinations (full house, straight, Yahtzee, etc.).
Bonus:

There's a bonus section on the scorecard. If the sum of your scores in the upper section (aces to sixes) is 63 or higher, you get a bonus of 35 points.
Yahtzee!

Rolling all five dice with the same number is a Yahtzee! This is a very valuable score.
The first Yahtzee you score is worth 50 points.
Any additional Yahtzees you roll after filling the Yahtzee box are worth 100 points each (bonus points!).
End of the game:

The game ends when all players have filled in all 13 boxes on their scorecards. Players then add up all their scores, including the bonus (if applicable). The player with the highest total score wins!

Tips:

Yahtzee is a game of chance and strategy. Think about the probabilities of rolling certain combinations and choose your scoring options accordingly.
Don't be afraid to re-roll your dice! You have three rolls to try and get the best possible score.
Pay attention to what other players are scoring. This can help you decide which boxes to fill in on your own scorecard.

2. Functions:

Roll Dice: This function simulates rolling the dice by generating random integers between 1 and 6 for each die.
Re-roll Dice: This function allows the user to select specific dice to re-roll (based on indices or positions) and updates the dice list.
Calculate Score: This function takes the current dice roll and a scoring category as input and calculates the corresponding score (e.g., sum for upper section categories, checks for specific dice combinations for lower section categories).
Update Scoreboard: This function allows the user to select a scoring category and updates the scoreboard with the calculated score. It should also check if the category has already been used.
Check End Game: This function determines if all categories on the scoreboard have been filled, indicating the end of the game.
3. Game Loop:

The core logic involves a loop that iterates through rounds. Within each round:

The user rolls the dice (using the Roll Dice function).
The user gets up to three re-rolls (calling the Re-roll Dice function if needed).
The user chooses a scoring category and the score is calculated (Calculate Score).
The user updates the scoreboard (Update Scoreboard).
The loop continues until all categories are filled or the user chooses not to re-roll anymore.
4. Additional Features:

You can add functionalities like displaying the current dice roll, showing the available scoring categories and their descriptions, and keeping track of the upper section bonus.
For a more user-friendly experience, you can implement a graphical interface or a text-based menu for interacting with the game.
Choosing a Programming Language:
